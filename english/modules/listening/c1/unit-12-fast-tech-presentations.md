# Unit 12: Presentaciones Tecnicas Rapidas — TED Talks y Conferencias a 160-180 WPM

## Objetivo

Comprender presentaciones tecnicas al ritmo natural de un hablante nativo preparado (160-180 palabras por minuto). En una charla TED o conferencia tecnica, el presentador habla RAPIDO, usa vocabulario especializado, construye argumentos complejos con datos, y tu necesitas tomar notas mientras escuchas. Este es el nivel mas alto de listening academico/profesional.

### Conectores de argumentacion compleja

Los presentadores tecnicos usan estos conectores para construir argumentos con datos:

| Conector | Funcion | Ejemplo |
|---|---|---|
| What this means is... | Traducir datos a significado | "Revenue grew 40%. What this means is we've doubled our market share." |
| Let me put that in perspective. | Contextualizar un dato | "50 terabytes. Let me put that in perspective — that's every book ever written, ten times over." |
| The key takeaway here is... | Resaltar la conclusion | "The key takeaway here is that speed matters more than features." |
| Now, here's where it gets interesting. | Transicion a dato sorprendente | "Now, here's where it gets interesting — the control group actually performed worse." |
| Contrary to what you might expect... | Introducir resultado inesperado | "Contrary to what you might expect, more options led to fewer sales." |
| The implications are significant. | Enfatizar importancia | "If this trend continues, the implications are significant." |
| Let me walk you through the data. | Iniciar analisis de datos | "Let me walk you through the data from our latest study." |
| To put it simply... | Simplificar algo complejo | "To put it simply, the algorithm learns from its mistakes." |
| The bottom line is... | Conclusion final | "The bottom line is, we need to invest now or fall behind." |

### Tecnicas de toma de notas mientras escuchas

#### El metodo Cornell adaptado para listening

Divide tu hoja en tres secciones:

```
+---------------------------+
| KEYWORDS    | MAIN NOTES  |
| (despues)   | (durante)   |
|             |             |
|             |             |
|             |             |
+---------------------------+
| SUMMARY (despues)         |
+---------------------------+
```

1. **MAIN NOTES (durante):** Escribe solo palabras clave y numeros. NO frases completas.
2. **KEYWORDS (despues):** Revisa tus notas y escribe las ideas principales.
3. **SUMMARY (despues):** Resume en 2-3 frases lo esencial.

#### Abreviaturas utiles para tomar notas

| Abreviatura | Significado |
|---|---|
| → | leads to, causes, results in |
| ↑ | increase, grow, rise |
| ↓ | decrease, drop, fall |
| ≈ | approximately |
| ∴ | therefore |
| vs | versus, compared to |
| w/ | with |
| w/o | without |
| b/c | because |
| esp. | especially |
| e.g. | for example |
| i.e. | that is |
| # | number |
| $ | cost, money, revenue |
| ? | question, unclear, verify |

### Presentacion simulada: Texto completo (3 minutos, ~500 palabras, ~170 WPM)

Lee este texto como si lo escucharas a velocidad nativa. Luego responde las preguntas.

---

**"The Hidden Cost of Decision Fatigue"**

Good morning, everyone. I want to talk to you today about something that's costing your company millions of dollars a year — and you probably don't even know it's happening. I'm talking about decision fatigue.

Now, here's where it gets interesting. The average knowledge worker makes about 35,000 decisions per day. That sounds like a lot, and it is. But the real problem isn't the number — it's what happens to the quality of those decisions as the day goes on.

Let me walk you through the data. In a landmark study by Danziger and colleagues, researchers analyzed over 1,100 judicial decisions made by Israeli judges over a ten-month period. What they found was shocking: the probability of a favorable ruling started at about 65 percent at the beginning of each session, and dropped to nearly zero just before a break. After lunch? Back up to 65 percent. The judges weren't biased — they were exhausted.

Let me put that in perspective. If you had your parole hearing at 9 AM, you had a 65 percent chance of being released. At 11:30 AM? Almost zero. Same judge. Same crime. Different time of day.

Now, what this means for the business world is profound. Your leadership team is making their most important strategic decisions in afternoon meetings — after they've already burned through thousands of micro-decisions about emails, Slack messages, and what to have for lunch. Contrary to what you might expect, the CEO isn't making better decisions because they're more experienced — they're making worse decisions because they're more depleted.

The key takeaway here is this: decision quality is a finite resource, not a skill. And we're wasting it.

So what's the solution? Three things.

First, front-load your critical decisions. Schedule your most important meetings before 11 AM. This alone can improve decision quality by up to 30 percent.

Second, reduce trivial decisions. This is why Steve Jobs wore the same outfit every day and why Mark Zuckerberg eats the same breakfast. They weren't being eccentric — they were preserving cognitive bandwidth.

Third, build decision frameworks. Instead of making each hiring decision from scratch, create a scorecard. Instead of debating every feature, build a prioritization matrix. The goal is to turn decisions into systems.

The bottom line is this: your brain is a battery, not a muscle. You can't train it to make more decisions. You can only be strategic about which decisions get your best energy.

The implications are significant. Companies that restructure their decision architecture — that front-load critical choices and systematize routine ones — see a 20 to 25 percent improvement in strategic outcomes.

Thank you.

## Repaso

1. Las presentaciones tecnicas van a 160-180 WPM — necesitas tomar notas, no intentar entender cada palabra
2. Los conectores de argumentacion ("What this means is...", "The key takeaway...") son tus ANCLAS — te dicen donde esta la informacion importante
3. Usa abreviaturas al tomar notas: →, ↑, ↓, b/c, w/, w/o, esp.
4. El metodo Cornell (notas durante + keywords despues + summary) es el mas efectivo para listening academico
5. "The bottom line is..." siempre introduce la CONCLUSION — si solo escuchas una frase, que sea esa
6. El vocabulario de presentaciones (leverage, scale, disruptive, actionable) se repite constantemente — aprendelo una vez, usalo siempre
7. Practica con TED Talks: primero con subtitulos en ingles, luego sin subtitulos, luego tomando notas
